Guiding Principles

Faith, Service, Integrity

After reviewing the materials gathered over the first two weeks of this course, I settled on three guiding principles that anchor both my spiritual and professional portfolios. Each has grown in meaning through specific scriptures, teachings, and experiences.

01

Faith

Faith is the foundation everything else in my portfolio rests on. Hebrews 11:1 describes faith as "the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not yet seen," which describes almost exactly where I stand as I plan a career in cybersecurity: I cannot yet see which opportunities will open, which challenges I will face, or what my work in Rimuka will ultimately accomplish. Doctrine and Covenants 88:118, "Seek learning, even by study and also by faith," has become a personal motto this term. It gives me permission to treat study and faith as one effort rather than two separate tracks, which matters in a technical field where it can be easy to assume that competence is purely a matter of skill and effort. In my portfolio, faith shows up less as a topic on its own page and more as the quiet confidence behind every project: that consistent, honest effort in something difficult will be honored, even when I cannot yet see the outcome.

02

Service

Service is the principle that turns my technical skills into something worth putting in a portfolio at all. John 13:34–35 and Doctrine and Covenants 4:2, "Serve him with all your heart, might, mind and strength," have reframed my IT and cybersecurity training as stewardship rather than simply a career track. The Parable of the Talents in Matthew 25:14–30 sharpened that further: my degree, my Technical Support Engineering certificate, and my growing cybersecurity knowledge are not mine to keep to myself, and burying them would be its own kind of failure. This principle drives my service-learning project in Rimuka, Kadoma, where I run basic computer literacy workshops and a digital fraud awareness campaign for people who are often the most vulnerable to the very risks I am learning to defend against. Elder Uchtdorf's teaching in "Lift Where You Stand" gives me language for this: the way I love God is shown most clearly in how I serve His children, including through something as ordinary as helping a neighbor recognize a phishing email.

03

Integrity

Integrity means doing the right thing even when no one is watching, and few fields make that as literal as cybersecurity does. Proverbs 11:3 teaches that "the integrity of the upright shall guide them," and 1 Corinthians 4:2 adds that "it is required in stewards, that a man be found faithful." Together these verses give me gospel language for something my future profession already demands: IT and cybersecurity professionals routinely have access to systems and data that belong to other people, and without integrity that access becomes a liability rather than a privilege. Proverbs 3:5–6 has also shaped how I think about integrity in planning: trusting the Lord does not mean I stop working hard or stop planning carefully, it means holding my plans loosely enough that I stay honest about what I do and do not know. I want integrity to be the trait that employers, clients, and the people of Rimuka can rely on without having to think twice.

Reflection Activity

Contemplative Notes

I set aside time this week for quiet reflection, thinking specifically about Elder David A. Bednar's image of gathering together "all things in Christ," and about how easily a project like this could split into two unrelated lists: spiritual items on one side, technical projects on the other. Sitting with that image, what stood out was how much the two lists actually overlap once I look closely. The same instinct that makes me want to patch a vulnerability before it is exploited is the instinct Proverbs 3:5–6 asks me to apply to my own life: address what I can see clearly now, and trust the Lord with what I cannot yet see. The same instinct that makes the Parable of the Talents meaningful to me is the instinct behind spending a Saturday teaching basic computer skills in Rimuka instead of only building my own resume. None of that was obvious to me at the start of this course; it became clearer specifically by slowing down and thinking about it rather than by adding another item to a list.

The clearest insight from this time of reflection is that my professional life will not be more spiritual by adding scripture references to a slide deck. It will be more spiritual by carrying the same character into both settings: patient with people who are still learning, honest about what I do not yet know, and willing to notice when a technically correct decision is not the same as a caring one. That has direct implications for how I want to run the Rimuka service-learning project going forward — not as a portfolio item to complete, but as an ongoing relationship with people who are trusting me with something real. It also changes how I think about my future career: the goal is not to keep a cybersecurity job and a discipleship separate and hope they do not conflict, but to let one continually inform the other, the way Elder Bednar describes intertwined strands rather than parallel tracks.

Professional Ethics

Case Study

The Equifax Data Breach, 2017

In 2017, Equifax, one of the largest credit reporting agencies in the United States, suffered a major cybersecurity breach after attackers exploited a known software vulnerability that had not been patched. As a result, the personal information of about 147 million people was exposed, including names, Social Security numbers, birth dates, addresses, and driver's license numbers.

Analyzing the situation against basic ethical and professional standards, the central failure was not a sophisticated, unpreventable attack; it was a known vulnerability that had a patch available and was not applied in time. This points to a breakdown in vulnerability management and accountability rather than a failure of technical skill. Professional frameworks such as the NIST Cybersecurity Framework treat timely patching and continuous monitoring as basic due diligence, not optional best practice, so the delay represents a departure from established professional norms rather than an unforeseeable event. There is also a question of transparency in the initial response: reports at the time noted that Equifax waited several weeks after discovering the breach before notifying the public, which raises a separate ethical concern beyond the technical failure itself, since the people whose data was exposed had no opportunity to protect themselves during that gap.

After the breach was discovered, Equifax investigated the incident, notified affected customers, and offered free credit monitoring services, later strengthening its cybersecurity systems and reaching settlements with government regulators. Those steps were reasonable and necessary, but they were a response to a failure that better patch management and monitoring could have prevented. Weighed against professional cybersecurity standards, Equifax's initial handling of the vulnerability fell short of due diligence, while the disclosure delay compounded the ethical problem by limiting affected individuals' ability to respond quickly. The case is a clear reminder that in cybersecurity, ethical failure is often a failure of ordinary discipline — patching on schedule, monitoring consistently, and communicating promptly — rather than a lack of technical knowledge.

As a future Information Systems and cybersecurity professional, this case reinforces that protecting information is both a technical and a moral responsibility. Installing security updates promptly, following established best practices, and communicating honestly and quickly when something goes wrong are not separate from technical competence; they are what technical competence looks like in practice when other people's information is at stake.
